# Static Image Prompt - Scenario Simulator

Use case: productivity-visual
Asset type: static website and consulting-deck illustration for the Scenario Simulator module
Target public asset after human generation: `images/scenario-simulator-planning-workshop.jpg`
Target format after conversion: high-quality JPG, 2000x1125 or larger, 16:9 landscape JPG
Primary audience: commercial vehicle product planners, strategy reviewers, dealer education teams, market research interviewers, fleet-service leaders, ESG reviewers, and AI-governance stakeholders.

## Core Request

Create a high-resolution, realistic but contemporary-art-inflected visual for a static GitHub Pages portfolio site called Future Commercial Mobility Strategy Lab. The image should look like an international-quality business design visual for product planning and market research, not a stock photo, not an official corporate system, and not a science-fiction concept poster. The setting is a calm customer strategy workshop where commercial vehicle planners compare urban delivery, cold-chain logistics, construction, bus operation, long-haul freight, fleet leasing, and closed logistics park scenarios on a large tabletop map. The visual should be bright, warm, trustworthy, professional, and grounded in a credible commercial vehicle planning context. It should support a strategy-lab website where future mobility signals are translated into cautious planning hypotheses, education materials, PoC candidates, roadmap timing, and human-review notes.

The image should feel practical and human-centered. Show product planners, a fleet customer representative, a service advisor, and a data-governance reviewer discussing route patterns, payload, charging access, downtime, ESG pressure, and infrastructure readiness. Their body language should suggest active reasoning, careful comparison, respectful listening, and cross-functional planning. Avoid heroic poses, sales-event gestures, exaggerated smiles, or staged advertising energy. The people should appear as professionals doing serious planning work in a bright room with real materials, papers, tablets, wall boards, and screens, while still allowing the image to have a refined contemporary-art quality through composition, light, surface texture, and subtle abstraction.

Include generic delivery vans, refrigerated box trucks, construction support trucks, buses, and long-haul tractors represented as small physical models or clean dashboard silhouettes; no brand shapes, badges, or license plates. The vehicles must be generic commercial vehicle forms only. They can be seen through windows, represented as scale models, shown in a depot bay, or rendered as clean silhouettes depending on the scene. They must not include Toyota, HINO, Hotai, competitor logos, dealer signs, official emblems, grille signatures that imitate a real manufacturer, or readable license plates. Do not use real customer names, government documents, official UI screenshots, real route names, or proprietary-looking records. The vehicle forms should support the product-planning context without turning the image into a brand advertisement.

## Dashboard And Planning Content

The scene may include scenario cards, route-type tiles, payload bands, depot-charging indicators, risk notes, human-review labels, and interview-question cards rendered as abstract planning artifacts. Any text inside the image must be minimal, secondary, and readable. If text is used, keep it in English only. Acceptable short words include "Scenario Simulator", "Demo Data", "Human Review", "Roadmap", "Scenario", "Energy", "Safety", "Fleet", "ESG", "PoC", or similarly short labels that support the visual theme. Do not include long paragraphs, dense charts, fake exact numbers, fake policy citations, confidential-looking data, or text that might imply official approval. Full explanations belong in the HTML i18n layer, not inside the image.

The image should communicate mechanism-based planning rather than prediction. If charts or UI fragments appear, make them abstract and illustrative. Show comparison, uncertainty, data confidence, constraints, customer suitability, route conditions, infrastructure readiness, service readiness, dealer education, and validation gates. Avoid visual language that says a technology is universally good, that a transition is inevitable, or that demo data proves a market fact. The dashboards should look useful for a portfolio prototype: refined, restrained, and organized, with enough detail to imply strategic reasoning but not so much text that the image becomes unreadable.

## Composition

Use three-quarter overhead workshop view, with the table and scenario map as the visual anchor and people arranged naturally around it. The composition should preserve stable negative space and a calm title-safe area: Top-right should have low detail for an optional short English label; keep main decision cards readable but abstract.. Prefer a 16:9 editorial frame that can be used in a responsive website card, hero area, or module image. Use layered depth: foreground planning artifacts, mid-ground people and vehicles, background context with windows, depot bays, or large screens. Let the image breathe. Keep the focal hierarchy clear: first the strategy work, then the commercial vehicle context, then the dashboard or roadmap details.

Use realistic camera logic: moderate wide-angle lens, natural perspective, eye-level or gentle three-quarter angle depending on the module, no extreme fisheye, no distorted vehicles, no impossible dashboard geometry. The scene can include subtle contemporary-art inflection through simplified shapes, translucent overlays, quiet geometric planning lines, and soft reflections, but it should remain believable as a professional strategy-lab photograph or editorial render. Avoid fantasy cityscapes, flying vehicles, holographic overload, dystopian lighting, cheap neon, black-red aggression, or overdramatic lens flare.

## Lighting, Mood, And Palette

Use soft daylight, believable interior lighting, warm reflective surfaces, and careful contrast. The visual should be comfortable to look at for a long time on a portfolio website. The preferred palette for this asset is morning-mist blue, warm ivory, mint green, light amber, and quiet coral for uncertainty markers. Color should support the four-theme website system without being dominated by a single hue. White and ivory areas should feel fresh rather than sterile. Green and blue should feel calm and strategic. Amber should suggest attention and planning warmth. Coral or red should appear only as restrained risk or validation cues, not as alarmist decoration.

The mood should be credible and reflective. This is a planning laboratory for commercial vehicle strategy, not a futuristic showroom. The image should suggest that the team is asking good questions about route predictability, payload, depot charging, service network, safety limitations, data governance, ESG reporting, policy verification, customer interviews, and roadmap validation. It should support the website's writing guardrails: careful, conditional, evidence-aware, and suitable for a product-planning interview portfolio.

## Materials And Detail

Use real-world materials such as light wood, matte glass, paper cards, whiteboards, fabric acoustic panels, brushed metal, soft plastic dashboard surfaces, and clean concrete or depot flooring where appropriate. Screens should have subtle glow, not harsh neon. Planning cards should have readable structure but not long text. The image should remain crisp at desktop hero size and still clear when cropped for mobile. Faces and hands should be natural and respectful. Avoid uncanny faces, extra fingers, incorrect vehicle wheels, impossible mirrors, broken dashboard text, and warped perspective.

Commercial vehicle details should be generic: clean cab shapes, cargo bodies, buses, vans, maintenance tools, charging posts, depot floor markings, and service bays. Avoid brand-specific grille patterns, badges, trademarked color bands, real license-plate formats, dealer flags, or recognizable showroom signage. If a vehicle needs a front view, make it deliberately generic with blank surfaces and no emblem zone.

## Negative Prompt And Exclusions

Do not include Toyota logos, HINO logos, Hotai Motor marks, real dealer marks, competitor marks, official corporate UI, official government documents, real customer records, real license plates, real route maps, QR codes, watermarks, signatures, hidden text, or photojournalistic depictions of a real company facility. Do not include accident scenes, injured people, surveillance aesthetics, aggressive policing, heavy cyberpunk HUDs, cheap neon, unreadable microtext, cluttered panels, fake confidential labels, militarized vehicles, luxury-car styling, or unrealistic autonomous-driving promises. Do not imply official endorsement, procurement recommendation, legal advice, engineering approval, verified carbon reduction, or product-launch schedule.

Avoid over-polished marketing stock imagery. Avoid generic smiling handshake scenes. Avoid dark blue monochrome dashboards, purple-blue gradients, beige-only monotony, or brown/orange dominance. Avoid crowded compositions where people, screens, vehicles, and charts compete equally. Avoid dramatic scale that makes the planning work look theatrical. Keep the image calm, warm, specific, and strategically useful.

The finished asset should look like a premium editorial render or carefully art-directed photograph for a serious portfolio website. It should be suitable for web hero use, strategy-module cards, and a consulting-deck slide. It must remain static, JPG-friendly, bright, human-centered, and brand-safe. ## Supplementary Production Direction

When generating the final image, treat the scene as a believable planning moment captured with editorial care. The image should not look like a generic corporate meeting, a product launch, or a futuristic vehicle advertisement. It should feel as though a product-planning team has paused in the middle of real strategic work: some materials are neatly arranged, some cards are being compared, and the dashboard or roadmap is useful but not theatrical. This balance matters because the website is a portfolio prototype for careful reasoning. The image should make the viewer feel that the team is comparing constraints, not declaring a fixed answer.

Use visual evidence of commercial vehicle planning without creating confidential or official-looking artifacts. Good elements include anonymous route cards, generic interview-question sticky notes, abstract service-readiness checklists, simple cost or timing bands, de-identified scenario markers, generic maintenance icons, and human-review stamps. Avoid documents that look like contracts, tenders, purchase orders, internal memos, engineering drawings, government letters, or customer records. If screens appear, they should be slightly simplified and intentionally non-specific, with clean layout blocks and short labels rather than detailed tables.

People should be treated with dignity and specificity. Avoid turning them into background decoration. Let one person listen, one point to a planning card, one compare a dashboard, and one take notes or facilitate review. Do not exaggerate diversity as a marketing trope; instead, show realistic cross-functional collaboration across product planning, service, customer research, ESG, safety education, and data governance. Clothing should be professional but ordinary. Faces should be natural and not overly retouched. The mood should feel cooperative, not celebratory.

Commercial vehicle forms should be accurate enough to read as fleet vehicles while remaining generic. Use plausible proportions, tires, cargo boxes, mirrors, maintenance bays, charging equipment, depot markings, and workshop details. Keep every grille, badge area, door, and plate zone blank or abstract. Do not use a recognizable manufacturer face, paint scheme, model name, dealer signage, or country-specific plate format. If a vehicle is partly cropped, make the crop intentional and useful for composition, not a way to hide brand-like errors.

The final output should be robust for responsive web use. Important people, vehicles, and planning artifacts should stay inside a central safe area so the image can crop gracefully on mobile. Keep the outer edges calmer, with fewer tiny labels and no critical content near the corners. The image should tolerate compression to JPG without losing meaning. It should still look professional after mild hover brightening, a 15 percent hover scale, or placement behind a rounded site container. Avoid brittle details such as tiny text, thin white lines on pale backgrounds, extreme depth-of-field blur over important information, or highly reflective screens that obscure content.

If the model produces unintended brand marks, unreadable text, distorted people, fake documents, or license-like plates, reject the output and regenerate with stricter blank-surface instructions. If the model creates a beautiful image that implies official deployment, autonomous certainty, verified carbon reduction, or a product-launch schedule, reject it even if the composition is attractive. The correct final image should support cautious strategy discussion, not overclaim it.
